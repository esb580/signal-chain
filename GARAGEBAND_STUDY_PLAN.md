## WEEKEND 1 — FOUNDATIONS

### SESSION 1 — Saturday Morning (2–3 hours)
**Topic: GarageBand Layout + Your First Real Track**

**Goals:**
- Know every section of the GarageBand window by name
- Record one clean 12-bar blues take over a drummer track
- Save and organize the project properly

**Practice tasks:**
1. Open GarageBand, create a new Empty Project at 72 BPM in E
2. Add a Drummer track — spend 15 minutes exploring the drummer editor
   (try different drummers, move the feel/complexity sliders)
3. Create an Audio track → set input to IR-200 → check levels
4. Record one full 12-bar take (don't stop even if you make mistakes)
5. Name the take, trim the silence, and save

**Concepts to understand after this session:**
- Tracks panel vs. the Arrangement area
- What the transport bar does (play, stop, record, cycle)
- Why you set tempo FIRST before recording anything
- The difference between an Audio track and a Software Instrument track

### SESSION 2 — Saturday Afternoon (2–3 hours)
**Topic: Multiple Takes, Editing, and Volume Matching**

**Goals:**
- Record 3–4 takes of the same 12-bar section and pick the best one
- Trim, align, and arrange regions
- Set proper relative volumes between drums and guitar

**Practice tasks:**
1. Open your Session 1 project
2. Record 3 takes of the rhythm guitar part (use Quick Punch: Command+R)
3. Compare takes: right-click region → "Open in Take Folder"
4. Pick the best take; trim start/end silence on the chosen take
5. Check alignment: does the chord change land on bar 5 beat 1?
6. Set drum track fader to -6 dB peak, guitar to -12 dB peak
7. Export as WAV — listen on headphones and speakers

**Concepts to understand after this session:**
- Take Folders and how GarageBand saves every pass
- How to nudge regions left/right for timing corrections
- The difference between the Fader (volume) and the Pan knob
- Why -6 dB headroom matters (prevents clipping when mastering)

### SESSION 3 — Sunday Morning (2–3 hours)
**Topic: Stereo Recording + The Full Signal Chain**

**Goals:**
- Record your guitar in stereo (both IR-200 outputs via RV-6)
- Understand how stereo panning works in GarageBand
- Hear the difference between mono and stereo guitar in a mix

**The stereo opportunity in your rig:**
Your IR-200 outputs A+B feed the RV-6 in stereo → RV-6 outputs stereo
reverb. This is genuinely wide, spacious sound. To capture it in GarageBand:

1. Create a **Stereo Audio Track** (New Track → Audio → select Stereo Input → IR-200 1+2)
2. Record your guitar — you'll see both L and R channels moving in the waveform
3. Pan this track to Center — the stereo information is already inside the file

**Stereo placement practice:**
1. Create a second rhythm guitar track (if you have a second part, or duplicate)
2. Pan rhythm guitar 1 to L-25, rhythm guitar 2 to R+25
3. Leave lead guitar at center
4. Bass at center, kick and snare at center
5. Listen — the stereo spread should feel wide and natural

**When stereo recording is worth it:**
- Lead guitar with your RV-6 plate reverb — YES, always, sounds enormous
- Rhythm comping with reverb — YES
- Dry rhythm guitar as a reference track — can be mono

**Concepts to understand after this session:**
- Difference between a mono audio file and a stereo audio file in GarageBand
- Why bass and kick always stay at center (phase and low-frequency physics)
- How the RV-6 stereo spread sounds in the mix vs. a mono reverb plugin

### SESSION 4 — Sunday Afternoon (2–3 hours)
**Topic: MIDI Drum Tracks + Building a Full Song Structure**

**Goals:**
- Import or create a MIDI drum track
- Arrange a full song: Intro → Verse → Chorus/Solo → Outro
- Understand how MIDI and Audio tracks work together

**MIDI drum track — the basics:**
GarageBand has two ways to get MIDI drums:

**Method A — Drummer Track (easiest):**
Already covered in Session 1. Use this by default.

**Method B — Software Instrument + MIDI Patterns (most flexible):**
1. New Track → Software Instrument
2. Choose Drum Kit Designer from the Library panel
3. I can generate MIDI patterns for you (see below) — import via drag-and-drop
4. Or use GarageBand's built-in **Beat Sequencer** (press B) to draw patterns

**Building song structure:**
Arrange your song in sections using the Arrangement Track:
- Track → Show Arrangement Track (at top of window)
- Click + to add sections: Intro / Verse / Chorus / Solo / Outro
- Color code them for visual clarity
- Each section can be 4, 8, or 12 bars

**Suggested simple blues structure:**
```
Intro (4 bars) → 12-bar Verse (12 bars) → 12-bar Solo (12 bars) → Outro (4 bars)
```

**Concepts to understand after this session:**
- MIDI is instructions, Audio is recorded sound — they mix together seamlessly
- How to loop/duplicate regions to fill song sections
- Why the arrangement track is your map of the song

## WEEKEND 2 — NEXT LEVEL

### SESSION 5 — Saturday Morning
**Topic: EQ and Light Compression (Mixing Fundamentals)**

You don't need heavy processing — your hardware rig sounds great.
But these two tools shape the final mix professionally.

**EQ — What to know:**
GarageBand's Channel EQ is on every track by default (click the Smart Controls area).

Blues guitar EQ starting points:
- High-pass filter at 80Hz on guitar (cut rumble/hum below that point)
- Slight boost at 2–3kHz (presence, cuts through the mix)
- Cut at 300–500Hz if it sounds muddy (often needed with Twin Reverb sim)
- Do NOT boost bass on guitar — your IR-200 already cuts bass aggressively

Drum track EQ:
- Often leave flat unless kick sounds thin (boost 80Hz) or cymbals are harsh (cut 8–10kHz)

**Compression — the one rule:**
Less is more. Compression evens out dynamics. Too much kills the feel.

Guitar compression starting point in GarageBand:
- Ratio: 4:1
- Threshold: -18 dB
- Attack: Slow (lets the initial pick attack through — that snap is important in blues)
- Release: Medium

**Practice task:**
Compare your Session 1 export to a newly mixed/EQ'd version. Can you hear the difference?

### SESSION 6 — Saturday Afternoon
**Topic: Recording Lead Over Rhythm — Layering Tracks**

The core skill: playing a lead guitar part on top of a rhythm track you already recorded.

**Setup:**
1. Open a project with a rhythm guitar track already recorded
2. Create a NEW Audio Track (don't record over the rhythm)
3. Solo the rhythm track so you can hear it while you play lead
4. Record your lead pass

**Lead guitar track settings:**
- If rhythm is panned L-25 → lead guitar sits at center or slight R
- Lead guitar gets a slight volume boost over rhythm (-10 dB vs -14 dB)
- Consider less reverb on rhythm, more reverb on lead (the spatial contrast sounds good)

**Tube Screamer on/off via the RC-1:**
Remember your rig setup: RC-1 → IR-200 (with Tube Screamer in Send/Return)
- Record the RC-1 loop output as your clean rhythm track
- Kick on the Tube Screamer for lead recording
- Both use the same tone base (Twin Reverb) but the overdrive colors only the lead pass

**Practice task:**
Record a complete 12-bar blues with:
- Drummer track
- A simple bass line (even a MIDI one from me)
- Rhythm guitar (clean, panned left)
- Lead solo (with Tube Screamer, center)

### SESSION 7 — Sunday Morning
**Topic: MIDI Bass Lines and Instrument Pairing**

I can generate MIDI bass patterns for you. Here's how to use them:

**In GarageBand:**
1. New Track → Software Instrument
2. Library → Bass → Classic Studio Bass (or any electric bass sound)
3. Open the Piano Roll (double-click a MIDI region or press Command+Option+P)
4. I can write the MIDI note data for any 12-bar blues key — ask me

**What good MIDI bass adds:**
- Locks the harmonic foundation with the drummer
- Frees your guitar playing from having to "cover all the space"
- Sounds professional because it's always perfectly in time

**MIDI + Audio pairing workflow:**
```
MIDI Drummer → MIDI Bass → Audio Rhythm Guitar → Audio Lead Guitar
(in time) → (in key) → (in tune, in feel) → (on top)
```

**I can generate for you:**
- 12-bar blues bass in any key (E, A, G, B)
- Shuffle or straight feel
- Slow blues (half-time feel) or uptempo
- Turnaround variations

Just ask and I'll write out the note pattern or provide the MIDI file.

### SESSION 8 — Sunday Afternoon
**Topic: Final Mix, Export, and Review**

**Full mix checklist:**
- [ ] Drums: -6 dB peak, center
- [ ] Bass: -10 dB peak, center
- [ ] Rhythm guitar: -14 dB peak, panned L-20 to L-30
- [ ] Lead guitar: -10 dB peak, center or slight R
- [ ] All tracks: EQ with high-pass at 80Hz applied
- [ ] Light compression on guitar tracks
- [ ] NO software reverb on guitar (your RV-6 handles it in hardware)
- [ ] Master volume at -6 dB headroom on output meter (don't clip the master)

**Export settings:**
- Share → Export Song to Disk
- Format: WAV, 24-bit, 44.1kHz
- This is archival quality — you can always convert down from here

**Review process:**
1. Listen on your studio speakers (the L+R monitors)
2. Listen on headphones (different frequency response — reveals things speakers don't)
3. Listen on a phone speaker (the lowest quality consumer experience)
4. If it sounds good on all three: you're done

## ONGOING PRACTICE AFTER STUDY PLAN

Once the two weekends are complete, these habits make every recording better:

**Before every session:**
- Set tempo first, always
- Lay down the drum track first, always
- Name your project and save before recording anything

**During recording:**
- One take at a time, name each region
- Don't try to edit while in the creative/recording flow
- Get the performance first, fix it later

**After recording:**
- Volume match before doing anything else
- EQ before compression
- Export the rough mix and listen a day later with fresh ears
